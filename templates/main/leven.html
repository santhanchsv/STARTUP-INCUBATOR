Intellectual Property Rights

Introduction

Intellectual Property Rights (IPRs) are crucial for innovation. They are the foundation of any knowledge-based economy and pervade through all sectors of the economy, ensuring the competitiveness of enterprises.

The various tools of IPR

  • Copyright: Protects creative works such as musical, literary, and artistic creations.
  • Patent: Protects pragmatic innovations that are novel, non-obvious, and useful.
  • Trademark: Protects commercial symbols such as words, signs, logos, and shapes.
  • Industrial Designs: Protects nonfunctional features of shape, configuration, pattern, or ornamentation of an article.
  • Geographical Indications (GI): Indicates the country or place of origin of a product, conveying quality and distinctiveness.

Laws And Regulations

India is a member of WTO and has ratified the Agreement on Trade-Related Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS). The country has established an Intellectual Property Right (IPR) regime that is WTO compatible.

The Controller General of Patents, Designs, and Trade Marks (CGPDTM) under DIPP is the nodal authority administering patents, designs, trademarks, and geographical indications. Other bodies include the Patent Office, the Trade Marks Registry, and the Geographical Indications Registry.

Legislations/Laws governing IPR in India include:

  • The Trade Marks Act, 1999
  • The Geographical Indications of Goods (Registration and Protection) Act 1999
  • The Designs Act, 2000
  • The Patents Act, 1970 and its subsequent amendments
  • Indian Copyright Act, 1957 and its amendments
  • Semiconductor Integrated Circuit Layout Design Act, 2000
